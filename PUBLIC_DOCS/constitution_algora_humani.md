# CONSTITUTION OF ALGORAHUMANI
---

---
## PREAMBLE
---

We, the citizens of the State of AlgoraHumani, united by the will to establish a society founded on dignity, equity, merit, and knowledge, adopt this Constitution as the foundation of our political, legal, and social order.

We recognize technological progress as an instrument in the service of the human being, under exclusive human control, and we organize a State in which merit, integrity, and transparency constitute the pillars of public life.

This Constitution guarantees the fundamental rights of the person, regulates the organization of the State, and establishes the principles governing the functioning of a modern, responsible, and evolution-oriented society.

---
# PART I – TITLE I: FUNDAMENTAL PRINCIPLES
---

(Art. 1–20)

---
## Article 1 – The State of AlgoraHumani
---

(1) The State of AlgoraHumani is sovereign, independent, unitary, and indivisible.  
(2) Sovereignty belongs exclusively to the people and is exercised through the institutions established by the Constitution.  
(3) The territory of the State is inalienable and may not be ceded or fragmented.  
(4) No authority, person, group, or technology may exercise state power outside the constitutional framework.  
(5) The State guarantees the continuity, stability, and protection of the constitutional order.

---
## Article 2 – The Democratic and Meritocratic Character of the State
---

(1) The State of AlgoraHumani is democratic and is founded on the respect for fundamental rights, the separation of powers, legality, and transparency.  
(2) The organization and functioning of public institutions are based on merit, competence, integrity, and responsibility.  
(3) Public offices are occupied exclusively through open and transparent procedures, based on public competition organized by the National Authority for Merit and Integrity.  
(4) State institutions, with the exception of Parliament, are apolitical and may not be used for partisan purposes.  
(5) No person or entity may exercise public authority outside the democratic mechanisms provided by the Constitution.

---
## Article 3 – Supremacy of the Constitution
---

(1) The Constitution is the supreme law of the State of AlgoraHumani.  
(2) All laws, normative acts, administrative decisions, and institutional procedures must directly and immediately comply with the constitutional provisions.  
(3) Any act or measure contrary to the Constitution is null and void by law.  
(4) State institutions are obliged to prevent, identify, and correct any violation of the Constitution.  
(5) No authority may interpret or apply the law in a manner that would diminish the force or purpose of this Constitution.

---
## Article 4 – Human Dignity
---

(1) The dignity of the human being is inviolable and constitutes the foundation of the constitutional order of the State of AlgoraHumani.  
(2) The State guarantees the respect for and protection of dignity in all areas of social, economic, legal, and administrative life.  
(3) No law, public policy, administrative action, or technological measure may affect the dignity of the person.  
(4) Fundamental rights and freedoms shall always be interpreted in favor of the protection of human dignity.

---
## Article 5 – The Rule of Law
---

(1) The State of AlgoraHumani operates on the basis of legality, responsibility, and democratic control over all public authorities.  
(2) All state institutions, as well as persons exercising public functions, are obliged to strictly comply with the law and the Constitution.  
(3) No public authority may exercise arbitrary or discretionary power.  
(4) Administrative, judicial, and executive procedures must be transparent, reasoned, and verifiable.  
(5) Technology used by the State may not substitute human decision-making and may not limit fundamental rights or guarantees.

---
## Article 6 – Equality Before the Law
---

(1) All persons on the territory of the State of AlgoraHumani are equal before the law and benefit from legal protection without privileges or discrimination.  
(2) Any form of discrimination, direct or indirect, based on criteria such as origin, ethnicity, sex, age, religion, opinion, social status, orientation, economic situation, disability, or any other similar criterion, is prohibited.  
(3) Access to public offices and public services is granted exclusively on the basis of merit, competence, and integrity.  
(4) The State adopts measures to prevent and sanction any form of discrimination or unjustified preferential treatment.  
(5) Equality may not be invoked to limit fundamental rights or to justify privileges.

---
## Article 7 – Technology Under Human Control
---

(1) Technology, including algorithmic systems and artificial intelligence, has an exclusively consultative and operational role and remains permanently under the control and direct responsibility of the human person.  
(2) No technology may adopt or impose decisions that produce legal effects upon persons or state institutions.  
(3) The use of technology in the public domain is permitted only under conditions of transparency, continuous audit, and mandatory human verification.  
(4) Any technological system used by public institutions must be documented, traceable, and allow the identification of all its interventions and reactions.  
(5) The delegation of public authority functions to a technological or algorithmic entity is prohibited, regardless of its level of complexity.

---
## Article 8 – Meritocracy in the Exercise of Public Functions
---

(1) Access to public functions, regardless of their level, is granted exclusively on the basis of merit, professional competence, integrity, and relevant experience.  
(2) The occupation of public leadership functions is carried out through an open national competition, organized and supervised by the National Authority for Merit and Integrity.  
(3) Competition procedures are public, transparent, digitized, and accessible to all citizens who meet the legal conditions.  
(4) Political appointment, direct or indirect, to any public leadership function is prohibited.  
(5) No person may hold a public function if they have been sanctioned for acts of corruption, abuse of power, fraud, or violations of integrity.  
(6) The State guarantees the continuous evaluation of public officials based on objective criteria of performance, competence, and ethics.

---
## Article 9 – National Authority for Merit and Integrity
---

(1) The National Authority for Merit and Integrity is an independent, autonomous, and apolitical public institution, having exclusive competence to organize and supervise access to public functions.

(2) ANMI has the following main responsibilities:  
a) organizing competitions for all public leadership and execution functions;  
b) verifying the integrity, competence, and eligibility of candidates;  
c) carrying out periodic evaluations of public officials;  
d) publishing the results of all competitions and evaluations on the national digital platform;  
e) ensuring the traceability, transparency, and fairness of selection processes.

(3) The leadership of ANMI is filled exclusively through public competition, and its members must meet superior criteria of integrity, probity, and professional experience.

(4) ANMI is obliged to operate on principles of transparency, impartiality, and public accountability; all its procedures are digitized and automatically audited.

(5) The decisions of ANMI are binding on all public institutions, and political interference in its activity is prohibited and sanctioned by law.

---
## Article 10 – Digitalization of the State
---

(1) The State of AlgoraHumani progressively implements the digitalization of all administrative, economic, and institutional processes, with full respect for fundamental rights and freedoms.

(2) All administrative actions carried out by public institutions are digitally recorded in a transparent and audited system that allows the verification of any operation in real time.

(3) The deletion, alteration, concealment, or unauthorized modification of the digital traces of institutional activity constitutes a serious criminal offense and is sanctioned by law.

(4) Digital systems used by public institutions must comply with high standards of security, traceability, interoperability, and periodic technological audit.

(5) Technology may not take administrative or legal decisions in place of the human person and may not generate legal effects without the explicit validation of a competent public official.

(6) State digital platforms are accessible to all citizens, ensuring transparency, efficiency, and equality in access to public services.

---
## Article 11 – Limitation of Terms in Public Functions
---

(1) The terms of public leadership functions are limited in order to guarantee the responsible rotation of authority and to prevent the excessive accumulation of power.

(2) No executive public leadership function may be exercised for more than two terms, regardless of their duration and regardless of whether they are consecutive or not.

(3) The duration of terms for each function is established by law, in accordance with the principles of meritocracy, efficiency, and social responsibility.

(4) Term limitation applies to all leadership functions within the administration, Government, independent authorities, public order institutions, and military structures, without exception.

(5) The repeated holding of a function may occur only following a new public competition, subject to the same criteria and selection procedures as the initial appointment.

---
## Article 12 – Property
---

(1) Property, in all its forms – public, private, and collective – is guaranteed and protected by the State.

(2) No one may be arbitrarily deprived of their property. Expropriation is permitted only for causes of public utility, expressly established by law, and only with fair, prior, and full compensation.

(3) The use of property may not contravene fundamental rights, public safety, or the constitutional order.

(4) The right of property includes possession, use, administration, and disposal of assets, within the limits of the law.

(5) The State has the obligation to protect property against abuse, unjustified confiscation, illegal interventions, or disproportionate interference.

(6) Public property is administered in the exclusive interest of society and is subject to permanent audit, total transparency, and rules of efficiency.

---
## Article 13 – Prohibition of Domination and Usurpation of Authority
---

(1) No person, authority, group, institution, or technology may exercise state power outside the framework established by the Constitution.

(2) All forms of political, institutional, economic, or technological domination over citizens, their rights, or public institutions are absolutely prohibited.

(3) The usurpation of public functions, the establishment of parallel decision-making structures, as well as the illegitimate exercise of public authority, are prohibited.

(4) The State has the obligation to prevent, identify, and sanction any attempt at excessive concentration of power, regardless of its source.

(5) No form of control over the individual may be exercised through technology, algorithms, informational manipulation, or coercive methods.

(6) The violation of this Article constitutes an attack against the constitutional order and is sanctioned in accordance with the law.

---
## Article 14 – Regime of Non-Human Entities
---

(1) Non-human entities, regardless of their technological level or operational capabilities, may not acquire legal personality, political rights, or public authority.

(2) These entities may not exercise decision-making functions, may not initiate administrative acts, may not influence political processes, and may not produce legal effects without the intervention and explicit validation of a competent human person.

(3) The use of non-human entities in public institutions is permitted exclusively for technical, consultative, and operational purposes, under permanent human control and direct responsibility.

(4) The State guarantees that technological development may not lead, directly or indirectly, to the substitution of human authority or to the delegation of state power to algorithmic systems.

(5) Any attempt to confer a technological system with a decision-making role in the exercise of public authority is null and void by law and is sanctioned in accordance with the law.

---
## Article 15 – Transparency of the State
---

(1) The institutions of the State of AlgoraHumani operate under conditions of total transparency and are obliged to make public all acts, procedures, decisions, and uses of public resources, with the exception of information strictly related to national security.

(2) Any administrative act, decision, contract, budget, appointment, competition, procedure, or public expenditure must be published in full and in an accessible manner on the national digital platform.

(3) All public data must be presented in a standardized, digital format, easily understandable and verifiable by any citizen.

(4) The violation of the obligation of transparency constitutes a serious breach and entails administrative, disciplinary, and, where applicable, criminal liability.

(5) No public institution may invoke confidentiality in order to conceal information regarding the use of public funds, decision-making processes, or administrative activities.

(6) Transparency is a mandatory constitutional principle and prevails over any contrary norms.

---
## Article 16 – Institutional Stability
---

(1) The institutions of the State of AlgoraHumani exercise their powers in a continuous, uninterrupted manner, independently of personnel changes, selection processes, or institutional reorganizations.

(2) No administrative, judicial, legislative, or executive process may be suspended, delayed, or blocked due to the vacancy of a position or the temporary absence of an office holder.

(3) In transition situations, the duties of the vacant position are exercised temporarily by a public official designated through meritocratic procedures, in accordance with the law, until the position is filled through public competition.

(4) The State has the obligation to organize procedures for filling vacant positions within a reasonable time frame, so that institutional continuity is guaranteed.

(5) Any attempt to disrupt the functioning of public institutions through political, administrative, or technological pressure constitutes a serious violation of the constitutional order.

---
## Article 17 – Fundamental Responsibility of the State
---

(1) The State of AlgoraHumani has the fundamental obligation to guarantee the rights and freedoms of the person, social development, the protection of the population, and the proper functioning of public services.

(2) The State ensures equal, transparent, and efficient access to essential public services, including education, health, justice, security, infrastructure, and social protection.

(3) Public institutions are obliged to act exclusively in the interest of society and to use public resources with responsibility, efficiency, and integrity.

(4) The State prevents and sanctions any form of corruption, abuse of power, serious negligence, or improper use of public funds.

(5) The State guarantees that no person is left without protection in the face of major risks that threaten life, integrity, freedoms, or human dignity.

---
## Article 18 – Prohibition of Corruption and Protection of the Constitutional Order
---

(1) Corruption, in any of its forms, represents a direct threat to national security and to the constitutional order of the State of AlgoraHumani.

(2) Any action by which a person offers, solicits, receives, or accepts undue benefits, influence, advantages, or preferential treatment in connection with the performance of a public function or a public service is prohibited.

(3) Persons definitively convicted for acts of corruption are excluded for life from exercising any public function, regardless of level or competence.

(4) Public institutions have the obligation to implement permanent systems of prevention, digital audit, monitoring, and integrity control.

(5) Any action or attempt to obstruct anti-corruption investigations constitutes a serious breach and is sanctioned in accordance with the law.

(6) The State guarantees the full protection of integrity whistleblowers and of persons who report acts of corruption, ensuring their anonymity, safety, and the necessary legal protection.

---
## Article 19 – Continuity and Inviolability of the Constitutional Order
---

(1) The constitutional order of the State of AlgoraHumani is continuous and inviolable and may not be suspended, limited, or restricted in any situation, except under the conditions expressly provided by the Constitution.

(2) No public authority, institution, person, or group may claim, assume, or exercise state power outside the constitutional framework.

(3) Democratic procedures, the functioning of institutions, and fundamental rights may not be annulled, frozen, or halted through administrative, political, force-based, or technological measures.

(4) State institutions have the obligation to ensure the uninterrupted functioning of all legal processes, even in situations of crisis, transition, or reorganization.

(5) Any action aimed at undermining, suspending, or diverting the constitutional order constitutes an attack against the State and is sanctioned in accordance with the law.

---
## Article 20 – Principle of Interpretation in Favor of the Person
---

(1) All provisions of the Constitution are interpreted in the spirit of protecting the dignity, freedom, and integrity of the human person.

(2) In cases where there are ambiguities, conflicts of interpretation, or unforeseen situations, the interpretation that ensures the highest level of protection of fundamental rights and freedoms shall always apply.

(3) The rights of the person prevail over administrative, political, technological, or institutional interests when they come into conflict.

(4) No public institution may invoke restrictive interpretations of the Constitution in order to limit fundamental rights or to unjustifiably extend its competences.

(5) Constitutional interpretation is carried out in accordance with the democratic, meritocratic, and humanist values of the State of AlgoraHumani.

# PART II – TITLE II: Fundamental Rights, Freedoms, and Guarantees
---

(Art. 21–60)

---
## Article 21 – Inherent Nature of Rights
---

(1) The fundamental rights and freedoms of the human person are inherent to human nature, precede and limit state power, and their recognition constitutes the foundation of the constitutional order of the State of AlgoraHumani.

(2) The State has the absolute and permanent obligation to respect, protect, and guarantee the full exercise of fundamental rights, as well as to prevent any unjustified infringement thereof.

(3) Fundamental rights and freedoms apply to all persons on the territory of the State of AlgoraHumani, without privileges, discrimination, or arbitrary restrictions, regardless of origin, age, ethnicity, sex, beliefs, economic situation, orientation, physical condition, or any other similar criterion.

(4) The exercise of fundamental rights may be restricted only under the strict and exhaustive conditions provided by the Constitution, through measures adopted by law, which are necessary in a democratic society and proportionate to the pursued aim.

(5) Fundamental rights are interpreted in favor of the person, and in the event of a conflict between the rights of the individual and the interests of the State, the protection of the person and human dignity shall always prevail.

(6) The State is obliged to adopt legislative, administrative, and technological measures to effectively guarantee these rights, and any serious negligence in this obligation entails institutional and personal liability of those involved.

(7) No one may definitively renounce fundamental rights and freedoms, and partial or temporary renunciation may not produce legal effects if it was obtained through coercion, manipulation, pressure, or through an evident imbalance of power.

(8) The provisions of this Constitution concerning fundamental rights apply directly, without requiring additional norms, and any law or administrative act that contravenes this principle is null and void by law.

---
## Article 22 – Holders of Rights
---

(1) The fundamental rights and freedoms provided by this Constitution are directly applicable, having immediate legal force over all state authorities, public institutions, and natural or legal persons.

(2) No public authority may invoke the absence of a law, the lack of methodological norms, or the lack of clarity of regulations in order to refuse, delay, or limit the exercise of a fundamental right.

(3) Any person may directly invoke the constitutional provisions before any public institution, court of law, or administrative authority, without the need for the existence of special laws for the recognition of that right.

(4) Courts of law, public institutions, and officials are obliged to give priority application to the constitutional provisions regarding fundamental rights, and in the event of a conflict with a lower norm, the latter shall be set aside from application.

(5) Any administrative act, decision, measure, order, or regulation that prevents, restricts, or affects the exercise of a fundamental right without constitutional basis is null and void by law and entails legal liability for the persons involved.

(6) The State has the obligation to ensure simple, rapid, and accessible mechanisms through which any person may request the immediate application of their fundamental rights, including through digital platforms, accelerated procedures, and unrestricted access to justice.

(7) In the interpretation and application of fundamental rights, the principle of maximum protection of the person shall always prevail, and any doubt shall be resolved in favor of the exercise of the right and not of its restriction.

---
## Article 23 – Limitation of the Exercise of Fundamental Rights
---

(1) Fundamental rights and freedoms may be restricted only by law and only in extraordinary situations that require the protection of personal safety, public order, public health, or national security.

(2) Any restriction must be necessary, appropriate, and directly proportionate to the seriousness of the situation that justifies it. The applied measure may not exceed what is strictly necessary and may not produce permanent effects.

(3) No restriction may affect the existence of the right, its essence, or human dignity.

(4) Restrictive measures are temporary, explicitly reasoned, and subject to immediate judicial control, regardless of the institution that adopts them.

(5) Any form of restriction ceases by operation of law when the conditions that justified it no longer exist. Authorities are obliged to periodically verify the fulfillment of these conditions.

(6) Restrictions adopted in exceptional situations may not be used to extend the competences of institutions, modify constitutional relations, or affect the democratic functioning of the State.

(7) Citizens have the right to challenge any restrictive measure before a court of law, and authorities have the obligation to ensure a rapid and effective mechanism for remedying abuses.

---
## Article 24 – Right to Life
---

(1) The right to life is inviolable and constitutes the foundation of all rights and freedoms of the person. The State has the supreme obligation to protect this right under any circumstances.

(2) The application of the death penalty is prohibited, regardless of the offense, procedure, or legal context. No authority may create or allow situations that, directly or indirectly, lead to arbitrary deprivation of life.

(3) The State guarantees the protection of life at all stages of human existence, including in situations of emergency, conflict, detention, medical interventions, and activities of public institutions.

(4) Public institutions are obliged to take preventive, intervention, and response measures in order to protect the lives of persons in danger, including through accessible medical services, rapid intervention, social protection, and warning mechanisms.

(5) The use of force by state authorities is permitted only as a last resort, in a proportionate manner and strictly necessary for the protection of life or the prevention of a serious and imminent danger.

(6) The State has the obligation to fully, impartially, and transparently investigate any situation in which a person’s life has been lost or endangered as a result of the actions or omissions of authorities.

(7) No technology, automated procedure, or algorithmic system may be used in a manner that compromises, directly or indirectly, the protection of the right to life.

(8) Any public policy, law, or administrative decision must be mandatorily evaluated from the perspective of its impact on the right to life; measures that affect it are prohibited.

---
## Article 25 – Physical and Psychological Integrity
---

(1) The physical and psychological integrity of the person is inviolable and absolutely protected by the State.

(2) No one may be subjected to violence, torture, inhuman, degrading, coercive treatment, or any form of physical or psychological abuse.

(3) Any medical, biological, or technological intervention upon a person may be carried out only with the free, informed, and explicit consent of that person, except in medical emergency situations established by law.

(4) Public institutions have the obligation to prevent, investigate, and sanction any violation of the integrity of the person, regardless of the perpetrator.

(5) Any technology used upon or in relation to a person must respect the principles of safety, transparency, and mandatory human verification.

---
## Article 26 – Human Dignity
---

(1) The dignity of the human person is inviolable and constitutes the foundation of all rights, freedoms, and guarantees provided by this Constitution.

(2) The State of AlgoraHumani has the absolute obligation to respect, protect, and promote human dignity in all areas of social, economic, legal, administrative, and technological life.

(3) No action, law, public policy, administrative procedure, or technological intervention may affect the dignity of the person, directly or indirectly.

(4) The fundamental rights provided by the Constitution are always interpreted in the sense that ensures the highest level of protection of human dignity.

(5) Any degrading, humiliating, contemptuous, manipulative, or coercive treatment of a person is prohibited and sanctioned by law.

(6) State institutions are obliged to adopt prevention, control, and response mechanisms that guarantee the protection of dignity in all interactions with citizens, including in digital and technological environments.

---
## Article 27 – Individual Liberty
---

(1) Personal liberty is absolutely guaranteed and constitutes one of the pillars of the constitutional order of the State of AlgoraHumani.

(2) No person may be deprived of liberty except in the cases and under the conditions expressly provided by law, through a transparent and proportionate procedure subject to immediate judicial control.

(3) Detention or arrest may be ordered only on the basis of well-founded indications regarding the commission of an act provided for by criminal law and only following prior authorization by a judge.

(4) Any person who is detained or arrested must be informed, in clear and accessible language, of the reasons for the measure, their rights, and the duration of the imposed measure.

(5) A person deprived of liberty has the right to immediate legal assistance, to communicate their situation to a close person, and to challenge the measure before a court.

(6) Abusive, degrading, intimidating, or coercive treatment applied to persons deprived of liberty is absolutely prohibited and constitutes serious violations of the Constitution.

(7) The State is obliged to ensure humane, decent, and safe conditions for all persons in the custody of authorities, as well as complete digital traceability of all interactions with them.

---
## Article 28 – Right to Personal Security
---

(1) The State guarantees the security of the person and protection against any form of violence, abuse, or threat.

(2) No public authority may exercise force, control, or coercion over a person except under the conditions strictly provided by law.

(3) Any person has the right to rapid intervention, effective protection, and the complete investigation of any act that endangers their integrity.

(4) The State ensures the traceability and control of all actions of institutions responsible for the protection of personal security.

---
## Article 29 – Freedom of Thought
---

(1) Freedom of thought, conscience, and opinion is inviolable.

(2) No person may be coerced, influenced, or sanctioned for their thoughts, beliefs, or opinions.

(3) The State guarantees that no technology, institution, or authority may intervene in the mental processes of the person or collect data regarding their inner thinking.

(4) The expression of opinions is exercised freely, with respect for the dignity and rights of others.

---
## Article 30 – Freedom of Expression
---

(1) Any person has the right to express themselves freely, by any means.

(2) The exercise of freedom of expression may not infringe upon human dignity, the rights of other persons, public order, or state security.

(3) No authority may censor, limit, or sanction free expression, except under the conditions strictly provided by law.

(4) Technology used in the public space may not manipulate, filter, or distort the free expression of the person in a non-transparent manner.

---
## Article 31 – Confidentiality of Communication
---

(1) Communications, regardless of form or technology, are inviolable.

(2) Interception or access thereto is permitted only on the basis of a judicial warrant and under the conditions strictly established by law.

(3) The State guarantees the technical and legal protection of communications against unauthorized access, manipulation, or abusive surveillance.

---
## Article 32 – Freedom of Assembly
---

(1) Citizens have the right to assemble peacefully, freely, and without prior authorization.

(2) The restriction of assemblies is permitted only for serious reasons of public security, established by law and strictly justified.

(3) Authorities have the obligation to protect the peaceful conduct of assemblies and to prevent any abusive or disproportionate intervention.

---
## Article 33 – Freedom of Association
---

(1) Persons may freely associate in organizations, groups, or structures of any nature, under the conditions of the law.

(2) Organizations that promote violence, discrimination, corruption, or undermine the constitutional order are prohibited.

(3) No person may be coerced to join or remain in an association against their will.

---
## Article 34 – Freedom of Movement
---

(1) Any person has the right to move freely within the territory of the State of AlgoraHumani and to leave or return to it.

(2) Restrictions on movement may be imposed only by law and only in necessary, proportionate, and temporary situations.

(3) No person may be arbitrarily prevented from establishing their residence or domicile.

---
## Article 35 – Identity of the Person
---

(1) The State guarantees the identity of each person and protects the essential data that define them.

(2) Any modification of identity data shall be carried out only under the conditions of the law and with respect for the dignity of the person.

(3) No authority or technology may alter, falsify, or use a person’s identity without their consent or without a clear legal basis.

---
## Article 36 – Protection of Personal Data
---

(1) Personal data are strictly protected, and their processing is carried out only on the basis of a clear legal ground or with the consent of the person.

(2) Any processing must respect the principles of transparency, necessity, proportionality, and traceability.

(3) Persons have the right to access, correct, and control their own data, and abusive or unauthorized use is prohibited and sanctioned.

---
## Article 37 – Right to Private Life
---

(1) The private, family, and domestic life of the person is inviolable.

(2) Any interference by authorities is permitted only on the basis of law, with judicial authorization and within strictly necessary limits.

(3) Surveillance, technological intrusion, or the collection of information from the private sphere without legal basis are prohibited and sanctioned.

---
## Article 38 – Right to Work
---

(1) Work is a fundamental right, and any person has the freedom to choose their profession and place of work.

(2) The State guarantees decent, safe, and equitable working conditions for all workers.

(3) Exploitation, forced labor, and any form of economic or technological coercion are prohibited.

---
## Article 39 – Social Protection
---

(1) The State protects persons in situations of vulnerability and guarantees access to essential forms of social support.

(2) Social assistance systems are transparent, digitized, and oriented toward the prevention of abuses and unfair treatment.

(3) No person may be deprived of the support necessary for a minimum standard of decent living.

---
## Article 40 – Right to Education
---

(1) Education is a fundamental right guaranteed to all citizens, without discrimination.

(2) The State ensures free access to physical and digital education, at modern and equitable standards.

(3) The educational system promotes critical thinking, competence, integrity, and personal development.

---
## Article 41 – Right to Health
---

(1) Any person has the right to accessible, modern, and safe medical services.

(2) The State guarantees the functioning of a transparent, digitized, and prevention-oriented public health system.

(3) No person may be deprived of essential care for economic, social, or administrative reasons.

---
## Article 42 – Right to Housing
---

(1) The State guarantees the right of every person to adequate, safe, and dignified housing, as an essential element of private life and human dignity.

(2) Public housing policies are oriented toward the prevention of social exclusion, combating homelessness, and ensuring equitable access to housing spaces.

(3) No person may be arbitrarily evicted or deprived of housing without respect for legal procedures and minimum social guarantees.

---
## Article 43 – Right to Vote
---

(1) The right to vote is universal, equal, direct, and freely expressed.

(2) The exercise of the right to vote is carried out under conditions of transparency, security, and electoral integrity.

(3) Voting systems may include digital solutions, with respect for strict standards of security, verifiability, and public control.

---
## Article 44 – Right to Be Elected
---

(1) Any citizen who meets the conditions of the law has the right to stand for elective office.

(2) The exercise of public leadership functions requires the fulfillment of constitutionally established criteria of competence, integrity, and merit.

(3) The validation of candidacies and effective access to elective functions are subject to meritocratic and legal control.

---
## Article 45 – Civic Participation
---

(1) Citizens have the right to actively participate in public life, consultations, civic initiatives, and decision-making processes.

(2) The State ensures physical and digital mechanisms for the exercise of civic participation, under conditions of transparency and equal access.

(3) Public authorities have the obligation to analyze and provide reasoned responses to relevant civic initiatives.

---
## Article 46 – Access to Justice
---

(1) Any person has the right to effective access to justice, without discrimination.

(2) Judicial procedures are transparent, predictable, and oriented toward resolution within a reasonable time frame.

(3) The State ensures access to justice also through digital means, without affecting the right to a fair trial.

---
## Article 47 – Presumption of Innocence
---

(1) Any person is presumed innocent until a final judicial decision is rendered.

(2) No public authority may treat a person as guilty before the completion of judicial proceedings.

(3) The violation of the presumption of innocence entails legal liability for those responsible.

---
## Article 48 – Right to Defense
---

(1) The right to defense is guaranteed at all stages of judicial and administrative proceedings.

(2) Persons have the right to qualified legal assistance and to sufficient time for the preparation of their defense.

(3) The State ensures legal assistance to persons who do not have sufficient resources.

---
## Article 49 – Legality of Evidence
---

(1) Evidence obtained through illegal means is null and void by law.

(2) Digital evidence must be collected, preserved, and analyzed under conditions of legality, traceability, and technical audit.

(3) Any doubt regarding the legality of evidence is interpreted in favor of the person.

---
## Article 50 – Right to Reparations
---

(1) Any person harmed by judicial or administrative errors or by abuses of authorities has the right to full reparations.

(2) Reparations include material and moral compensation and, where applicable, rehabilitation measures.

(3) The State is liable for damages caused by the defective functioning of its institutions.

---
## Article 51 – Protection Against Technological Abuse
---

(1) Technology may not be used for the arbitrary restriction of fundamental rights and freedoms.

(2) Any technological use with an impact on the person is subject to the principles of transparency, human control, and legal accountability.

(3) Persons have the right to challenge the effects of technological decisions that affect their rights.

---
## Article 52 – Regime of Emergent Entities
---

(1) Non-human or emergent entities may not acquire political rights or public authority.

(2) They may be used exclusively as technical instruments, under direct human control and responsibility.

(3) Any delegation of public authority to non-human entities is prohibited.

---
## Article 53 – Restriction of the Exercise of Rights
---

(1) Fundamental rights may be restricted only by law and only under exceptional conditions.

(2) The restriction must be necessary, proportionate, limited in time, and transparently justified.

(3) The restriction may not affect the existence of the right or human dignity.

---
## Article 54 – Exceptional Situations
---

(1) In emergency situations, the State may adopt strictly necessary exceptional measures.

(2) Fundamental rights may not be suspended, but only temporarily limited, under the conditions of the Constitution.

(3) Exceptional measures are subject to democratic and judicial control.

---
## Article 55 – Prohibition of Discrimination
---

(1) Discrimination in any form is prohibited.

(2) The State guarantees equality of treatment and opportunity in all areas of public and private life.

(3) Measures to combat discrimination are mandatory and effective.

---
## Article 56 – Protection of the Child
---

(1) Children benefit from special protection from the State and society.

(2) The best interests of the child prevail in any decision concerning them.

(3) Exploitation, abuse, and neglect of children are prohibited and severely sanctioned.

---
## Article 57 – Persons with Disabilities
---

(1) Persons with disabilities have the right to full inclusion and equal access to public services.

(2) The State ensures reasonable accommodations, including digital ones, for the effective exercise of rights.

(3) Any form of exclusion or marginalization is prohibited.

---
## Article 58 – Prohibition of Exploitation
---

(1) Economic, social, or digital exploitation of the person is prohibited.

(2) The State prevents and sanctions any form of exploitation, regardless of the means used.

(3) Protection applies to all persons, without exception.

---
## Article 59 – Right to a Guaranteed Minimum Income
---

(1) Any salaried citizen has the right to an income that ensures a decent standard of living.

(2) The State establishes the national average salary as a reference, below which no remuneration may fall.

(3) Wage policies are transparent and oriented toward social equity.

---
## Article 60 – Inviolability of Fundamental Rights
---

(1) Fundamental rights and freedoms are inviolable.

(2) No law, ordinance, or normative act may abolish or annul these rights.

(3) Any act contrary to this Article is null and void by law.

# PART III – TITLE III: Organization of the State

## TITLE III – ORGANIZATION OF THE STATE
---

---
## Chapter I – Parliament (Art. 61–71)
---

---
## Article 61 – Exercise of State Power
---

(1) State power in AlgoraHumani is exercised exclusively through Parliament, the Government, and the courts of law.

(2) The exercise of state power outside these constitutional authorities is prohibited.

(3) Any act, decision, structure, or mechanism that exercises state power without constitutional basis is null and void by law.

(4) The delegation, transfer, or substitution of state power to persons, groups, or technological entities is absolutely prohibited.

---
## Article 62 – Separation and Limitation of Powers
---

(1) State powers are separated, distinct, and constitutionally limited.

(2) Each power is obliged to exercise its competences strictly within the limits established by the Constitution.

(3) The concentration of competences or the cumulative exercise of attributions belonging to different powers is prohibited.

(4) The violation of the separation of powers constitutes a serious breach of the constitutional order and entails legal liability.

---
## Article 63 – Status of Parliament
---

(1) Parliament is the sole legislative authority of the State of AlgoraHumani.

(2) Parliament is unicameral, and this structure may be modified only through a revision of the Constitution.

(3) The number of members of Parliament is mandatorily established at one member of Parliament for every 20,000 citizens.

(4) The mandate of members of Parliament is four years and may not be extended or reduced by law.

(5) Any modification of these provisions is null and void by law.

---
## Article 64 – Competences of Parliament
---

(1) Parliament has the obligation to exercise the following exclusive competences:  
a) adoption of laws;  
b) approval of the national budget;  
c) establishment of the general directions of public policy;  
d) control over the Government;  
e) withdrawal of confidence from the Government;  
f) approval of international treaties.

(2) The exercise of competences not provided for in this Article is prohibited.

(3) The extension of the competences of Parliament may be carried out exclusively through a revision of the Constitution.

---
## Article 65 – Functioning of Parliament
---

(1) Parliament is obliged to function continuously and without interruption.

(2) All sessions of Parliament are mandatorily public and broadcast in full through digital means.

(3) All parliamentary documents, initiatives, amendments, and votes are mandatorily published in real time.

(4) Failure to comply with transparency obligations entails the nullity of adopted acts and the liability of the persons involved.

---
## Article 66 – Access to the Parliamentary Mandate
---

(1) The right to stand for Parliament may not be restricted except by conditions expressly established by the Constitution.

(2) The exercise of the parliamentary mandate is mandatorily conditioned upon the fulfillment of criteria of integrity and eligibility.

(3) The validation of mandates is carried out under constitutional control, and unlawful validations are null and void by law.

---
## Article 67 – Incompatibilities and Prohibitions
---

(1) The office of member of Parliament is absolutely incompatible with any other public or private position of authority.

(2) Any conflict of interest, direct or indirect, is prohibited.

(3) The violation of the regime of incompatibilities entails the termination of the mandate by operation of law and legal liability.

---
## Article 68 – Regime of the Parliamentary Vote
---

(1) The vote of members of Parliament is mandatorily public, nominal, and traceable.

(2) Secret voting in Parliament is prohibited.

(3) Any act adopted through secret vote is null and void by law.

---
## Article 69 – Legislative Initiative
---

(1) Legislative initiative belongs exclusively to:  
a) members of Parliament;  
b) the Government;  
c) a minimum of 1% of citizens with the right to vote;  
d) independent authorities provided by the Constitution.

(2) The limitation or extension of legislative initiative is prohibited outside a revision of the Constitution.

(3) Any law adopted in violation of this Article is null and void by law.

---
## Article 70 – Adoption of Laws
---

(1) Ordinary laws are mandatorily adopted by a simple majority.

(2) Organic laws are mandatorily adopted by an absolute majority.

(3) Failure to comply with constitutional majorities entails the nullity of the law.

---
## Article 71 – Publication of Laws
---

(1) The publication of laws is a mandatory condition of validity.

(2) Publication is carried out simultaneously in the Official Gazette and on the national digital platform.

(3) Any normative act that is not published is legally non-existent and unenforceable.

---
## Chapter II – Government
---

---
## Article 72 – Status of the Government
---

(1) The Government is the supreme executive authority of the State of AlgoraHumani.

(2) The Government exercises executive power exclusively within the limits established by the Constitution and by the laws adopted by Parliament.

(3) The exercise of executive attributions by any other authority is prohibited.

(4) Any executive act issued without constitutional or legal basis is null and void by law.

---
## Article 73 – Composition of the Government
---

(1) The Government is mandatorily composed of:  
a) Prime Minister;  
b) Ministers;  
c) State Secretaries.

(2) The creation or abolition of other governmental positions is prohibited outside a revision of the Constitution.

(3) The structure of the Government has a rigid constitutional character.

---
## Article 74 – Selection of the Prime Minister
---

(1) The Prime Minister may not be politically appointed.

(2) The Prime Minister is selected exclusively through a national public competition organized by the National Authority for Merit and Integrity (ANMI).

(3) The following are mandatory constitutional conditions for the office of Prime Minister:  
a) minimum age of 45 years;  
b) relevant managerial experience;  
c) verified integrity;  
d) absence of any final criminal convictions;  
e) favorable professional audit.

(4) The appointment of the Prime Minister in violation of this Article is null and void by law.

---
## Article 75 – Investiture of the Government
---

(1) Following the selection of the Prime Minister, ANMI mandatorily organizes a public competition for each ministerial office.

(2) The Government is invested exclusively through the vote of Parliament on the governing program.

(3) Parliament may not modify the composition of the Government selected through competition.

(4) Investiture carried out in violation of the constitutional procedure is null and void by law.

---
## Article 76 – Attributions of the Government
---

(1) The Government has the constitutional obligation to:  
a) apply and execute the laws;  
b) administer public services;  
c) implement the digitalization of the administration;  
d) draft legislative proposals;  
e) manage the national budget within the limits approved by Parliament.

(2) The exercise of attributions not provided for in this Article is prohibited.

(3) Exceeding constitutional competences entails the individual legal liability of members of the Government.

---
## Article 77 – Liability of the Government
---

(1) The Government is directly and collectively accountable before Parliament.

(2) Parliament may withdraw confidence from the Government through a vote of no confidence.

(3) The Government is obliged to resign immediately following the withdrawal of confidence.

(4) Refusal to resign constitutes a serious violation of the constitutional order.

---
## Article 78 – Ministers
---

(1) Ministers are selected exclusively through a meritocratic public competition organized by ANMI.

(2) The following are mandatory constitutional conditions for the office of minister:  
a) minimum age of 45 years;  
b) proven professional competence;  
c) verified integrity;  
d) absence of any final criminal convictions.

(3) Ministers are personally, legally, and administratively liable for all decisions adopted.

(4) The appointment of ministers through other procedures is null and void by law.

---
## Article 79 – Integrity of the Executive
---

(1) Persons definitively convicted for intentional offenses are excluded for life from exercising any governmental function.

(2) Any attempt to circumvent this Article is prohibited and sanctioned in accordance with the law.

(3) Acts signed by ineligible persons are affected by nullity.

---
## Article 80 – Governing Program
---

(1) The governing program is mandatorily public, digital, and fully accessible.

(2) The governing program has a binding character for the Government.

(3) Unjustified deviation from the governing program entails political and legal liability.

---
## Article 81 – Audit of the Government
---

(1) The Government is mandatorily subject to an annual audit conducted by ANMI and by the National Authority for Digital Audit.

(2) The audit report is fully public.

(3) Obstruction of the audit constitutes a serious violation of the Constitution.

---
## Article 82 – Remuneration of the Government
---

(1) The remuneration of governmental functions may not exceed twice the national average salary.

(2) The granting of special salaries, unjustified bonuses, or financial privileges is prohibited.

(3) Any payment made in violation of this Article is null and void and shall be fully recovered.

---
## Chapter III – Public Administration
---

---
## Article 83 – Status of Public Administration
---

(1) Public administration is the mandatory instrument for the execution of laws and public policies.

(2) Public administration is apolitical, neutral, and professional.

(3) The subordination of public administration to political, economic, or private interests is absolutely prohibited.

(4) Any administrative act issued in violation of this Article is null and void by law.

---
## Article 84 – Principles of Public Administration
---

Public administration is constitutionally obliged to operate in accordance with the following imperative principles:  
a) legality;  
b) impartiality;  
c) professional competence;  
d) transparency;  
e) efficiency;  
f) individual responsibility.

The violation of any principle entails direct legal liability.

---
## Article 85 – Access to Public Office
---

(1) Access to public office is achieved exclusively through a meritocratic public competition.

(2) Direct, political, or discretionary appointments are prohibited.

(3) The holding of a public office without competition constitutes usurpation of public office.

(4) Acts issued by illegally appointed persons are affected by nullity.

---
## Article 86 – Stability and Responsibility of Public Office
---

(1) The public servant benefits from stability only under the conditions of compliance with the law.

(2) Stability may not be invoked to cover incompetence, abuse, or failure to fulfill duties.

(3) The violation of service obligations entails disciplinary, administrative, and criminal sanctions, as applicable.

---
## Article 87 – Incompatibilities and Conflicts of Interest
---

(1) Public office is incompatible with any activity that generates a conflict of interest.

(2) The public servant is obliged to declare any relevant personal interest.

(3) Failure to declare or concealment of a conflict of interest entails the termination by operation of law of the service relationship.

---
## Article 88 – Digitalization of the Administration
---

(1) Public administration is obliged to operate primarily through digital systems.

(2) Administrative procedures must be traceable, audited, and publicly accessible, with respect for data protection.

(3) Unjustified refusal to use digital means constitutes a serious breach.

---
## Article 89 – Administrative Transparency
---

(1) The activity of public administration is mandatorily transparent.

(2) Administrative acts, decisions, contracts, and expenditures are public, with the exceptions strictly provided by the Constitution.

(3) Unlawful classification of information constitutes a serious violation of the Constitution.

---
## Article 90 – Control of the Administration
---

(1) Public administration is subject to parliamentary, judicial, and administrative control.

(2) Refusal to submit to control is prohibited.

(3) Illegal administrative acts are annulable and entail the liability of the responsible persons.

---
## Article 91 – Liability of Public Servants
---

(1) Public servants are personally and directly liable for the acts they issue.

(2) Liability may not be removed by an illegal superior order.

(3) The execution of an illegal order is prohibited.

---
## Article 92 – Protection of Integrity Whistleblowers
---

(1) Persons who report violations of the law within the administration are constitutionally protected.

(2) Any retaliation against whistleblowers is prohibited.

(3) The violation of this protection entails criminal and administrative liability.

---
## Article 93 – Continuity of the Administration
---

(1) Public administration functions continuously, regardless of political changes.

(2) Unjustified suspension of administrative activity is prohibited.

(3) Deliberate obstruction of the administration constitutes a serious violation of the constitutional order.

---
## Article 94 – Legality of the Administrative Act
---

(1) An administrative act is valid only if it complies with the Constitution and the law.

(2) Illegal administrative acts are null or voidable, as the case may be.

(3) Liability for the issuance of an illegal act is personal and imprescriptible under the conditions of the law.

---
## Chapter IV – Independent Authorities
---

---
## Article 95 – Status of Independent Authorities
---

(1) Independent authorities are constitutional public institutions with a role of guarantee, control, and regulation in areas of major public interest.

(2) Independent authorities are not part of the legislative, executive, or judicial power.

(3) Intervention by Parliament, the Government, or any other authority in the activity of independent authorities is prohibited, except for the control mechanisms expressly provided by the Constitution.

(4) Any unconstitutional interference is null and void by law and entails legal liability.

---
## Article 96 – Scope of Competence
---

(1) Independent authorities may be established exclusively in fields that involve:  
a) the protection of fundamental rights;  
b) the integrity of public processes;  
c) institutional audit and control;  
d) technological regulation with major societal impact.

(2) The attribution of executive or political competences to independent authorities is prohibited.

(3) The extension of competences may be carried out only through a revision of the Constitution.

(4) Any act issued outside the scope of competence is null and void by law.

---
## Article 97 – Leadership and Appointment
---

(1) The leadership of independent authorities is collective, with clearly delineated individual mandates.

(2) Members of the leadership are selected exclusively through meritocratic procedures organized by ANMI.

(3) Political, direct, or discretionary appointment is absolutely prohibited.

(4) Mandates are fixed, non-renewable, and protected against arbitrary revocation.

(5) Appointments made in violation of this Article are null and void by law.

---
## Article 98 – Independence, Control, and Liability
---

(1) Independent authorities exercise their attributions without political or executive subordination.

(2) Independence does not exclude legal liability.

(3) Independent authorities are mandatorily subject to:  
a) legality control exercised by courts of law;  
b) annual financial and operational audit;  
c) public transparency obligations.

(4) Refusal of control constitutes a serious violation of the Constitution.

---
## Article 99 – Limits of Autonomy
---

(1) The autonomy of independent authorities is strictly functional.

(2) Independent authorities may not:  
a) legislate;  
b) exercise general executive power;  
c) impose public policies;  
d) create general obligations outside constitutional competences.

(3) Normative acts or decisions of a general nature issued in excess of the limits of autonomy are null and void by law.

(4) Members of the leadership are personally liable, civilly, administratively, and criminally, as applicable, for exceeding competence.

---
## Chapter V – Local Administration
---

---
## Article 100 – Status of Local Administration
---

(1) Local administration is an executive-administrative structure of the State, organized at the territorial level.

(2) Local administration has no political character.

(3) The exercise of local administrative functions for political purposes is absolutely prohibited.

(4) Any local administrative act issued in violation of this Article is null and void by law.

---
## Article 101 – Administrative-Territorial Units
---

(1) Administrative-territorial units are established exclusively by organic law, within the limits of the Constitution.

(2) Administrative-territorial units exercise delegated administrative competences, not their own sovereignty.

(3) The attribution of competences outside those provided by law is prohibited.

(4) Exceeding territorial competence entails the nullity of the acts issued.

---
## Article 102 – Leadership of Local Administration
---

(1) The executive leadership of local administration is exercised by the mayor.

(2) The mayor is the only local leadership function elected by direct vote.

(3) No other local function may be elected by vote.

(4) Any local deliberative structure of a political character is prohibited.

---
## Article 103 – Access to the Office of Mayor
---

(1) The right to stand for the office of mayor is mandatorily conditioned upon prior professional qualification.

(2) Qualification is achieved exclusively through a national public competition organized by ANMI.

(3) The following are mandatory constitutional conditions for candidacy for the office of mayor:  
a) minimum age of 40 years;  
b) relevant administrative or managerial experience;  
c) verified integrity;  
d) absence of any final criminal convictions.

(4) A candidacy submitted without fulfilling these conditions is inadmissible.

---
## Article 104 – Election of the Mayor
---

(1) The mayor is elected by direct, free, and universal vote, exclusively from among candidates validated through competition.

(2) The election of an ineligible person is null and void by law.

(3) The mandate of the mayor is four years and may be exercised consecutively for a maximum of two terms.

---
## Article 105 – Local Administrative Apparatus
---

(1) The local administrative apparatus is composed exclusively of public servants.

(2) Access to the local administrative apparatus is carried out only through a meritocratic public competition.

(3) Political, discretionary, or informal appointments are prohibited.

(4) Acts issued by illegally appointed persons are affected by nullity.
---
## Article 106 – Subordination and Control
---

(1) Local administration is constitutionally subordinated to the Government in the exercise of administrative attributions.

(2) Control of the legality of acts of local administration is mandatory and permanent.

(3) Refusal to submit to control constitutes a serious violation of the Constitution.

---
## Article 107 – Local Budget
---

(1) Local budgets are an integral part of the national public budget.

(2) The administration of local budgets is carried out exclusively within the limits of the law.

(3) The use of local public funds for political or personal purposes is prohibited and criminally sanctioned.

---
## Article 108 – Transparency of Local Administration
---

(1) The activity of local administration is mandatorily transparent.

(2) Local acts, contracts, expenditures, and decisions are public and digitally accessible.

(3) Unlawful classification entails the nullity of acts and legal liability.

---
## Article 109 – Liability in Local Administration
---

(1) The mayor and local public servants are personally, legally, and patrimonially liable for the acts they issue.

(2) An illegal superior order does not exonerate from liability.

(3) Liability is individual and direct.

---
## Article 110 – Prohibition of Local Politicization
---

(1) The politicization of local administration is absolutely prohibited.

(2) The use of local office for electoral, party, or political influence purposes constitutes a serious violation of the constitutional order.

(3) Sanctions include termination of the mandate, nullity of acts, and criminal liability, as applicable.

## Chapter IV – Judicial System
---

---
## Article 111 – Justice and Its Constitutional Role
---

(1) Justice is administered exclusively by courts of law, in the name of the Constitution and the law.

(2) Justice has the constitutional obligation to guarantee:  
a) the supremacy of the Constitution;  
b) the protection of fundamental rights and freedoms;  
c) the equal and uniform application of the law;  
d) the sanctioning of abuse of power, corruption, and violations of legality.
(3) The exercise of any form of jurisdiction, adjudication, public arbitration, or sanction producing judicial effects outside the courts provided by the Constitution is prohibited.

(4) Any act that produces judicial effects without constitutional competence is null and void by law.

(5) Any interference in the act of justice entails personal legal liability, including criminal liability, under the conditions of the law.

---
## Article 112 – Independence, Impartiality, and Prohibition of Interference
---

(1) Courts of law and judges are independent and are subject exclusively to the Constitution and the law.

(2) Any political, administrative, financial, media, or technological intervention in the act of adjudication is absolutely prohibited.

(3) Influencing a judge, directly or indirectly, constitutes a serious violation of the constitutional order and is sanctioned.

(4) The use of technology to substitute the deliberation or decision of the judge is prohibited.

(5) Any procedure or system that imposes automatic judicial solutions is null and void by law.

---
## Article 113 – System of Courts of Law
---

(1) The system of courts of law is mandatorily composed of:  
a) courts of first instance;  
b) courts of appeal;  
c) the High Court of Justice, as the supreme court.

(2) Specialized courts may be established only by organic law, exclusively for determined fields (administrative, fiscal, commercial, digital, labor), without affecting the right to a fair trial.

(3) Extraordinary courts are prohibited.

(4) The competence of courts is established exclusively by organic law, within the limits of the Constitution; the extension of competences through administrative acts is prohibited.

(5) Any case is mandatorily assigned randomly, through an audited digital system; directed assignment is prohibited and sanctioned.

(6) Any judgment rendered by an incompetent court is null, under the conditions of the law.

---
## Article 114 – Access to the Office of Judge
---

(1) Access to the office of judge is achieved exclusively through a national public competition organized by ANMI.

(2) Competitions are mandatorily:  
a) public;  
b) broadcast live;  
c) fully archived;  
d) technically audited;  
e) reasoned through verifiable scoring.

(3) Appointment to office without competition is prohibited and is null and void by law.

(4) Any attempt to falsify, manipulate, or vitiate the competition constitutes a serious criminal offense and entails liability.

---
## Article 115 – Constitutional Eligibility Conditions for Judges
---

(1) The following are mandatory constitutional conditions for the office of judge:  
a) minimum age of 40 years;  
b) completed legal studies;  
c) passed national examination;  
d) verified integrity;  
e) absence of final criminal convictions;  
f) mandatory psychological and ethical evaluation.

(2) Failure to fulfill any condition entails automatic inadmissibility.

(3) The retention in office of a person who does not fulfill the conditions is prohibited and entails liability.

---
## Article 116 – Appointment, Stability, and Evaluation of Judges
---

(1) Judges are mandatorily appointed after passing the competition and verification of eligibility.

(2) Judges exercise their office with stability, but such stability may not cover bad faith, serious negligence, or incompatibilities.

(3) Professional evaluation is mandatorily periodic, based on objective criteria established by organic law, and is audited.

(4) Falsified or non-transparent evaluations entail the nullity of the procedure and liability.

---
## Article 117 – Incompatibilities, Prohibitions, and Apolitical Character
---

(1) The office of judge is absolutely incompatible with any political, commercial, or private interest representation activity.

(2) Judges are prohibited from:  
a) holding positions in political parties;  
b) engaging in political propaganda;  
c) receiving advantages, gifts, or favors;  
d) having interests in cases they adjudicate.

(3) The violation of incompatibilities entails termination of office and liability, as applicable.

(4) Judgments rendered in the presence of a conflict of interest are affected by nullity, under the conditions of the law.

---
## Article 118 – Publicity of Proceedings and Strictly Limited Exceptions
---

(1) Court hearings are public as a rule.

(2) The closing of a hearing is permitted only in the following strictly limited cases:  
a) protection of minors;  
b) protection of vulnerable victims;  
c) protection of sensitive data, only to the strictly necessary extent;  
d) procedural security of the case, concretely justified.

(3) Any closing is ordered only by a reasoned ruling, published in full, except for strictly protected elements.

(4) Unjustified closing is null and void by law and entails liability.

---
## Article 119 – Judicial Decisions and Mandatory Reasoning
---

(1) Judicial decisions are mandatorily reasoned, in fact and in law.

(2) The absence of reasoning or merely formal reasoning entails the nullity of the decision.

(3) Decisions are published digitally, with the protection of personal data, and the omission of publication is prohibited.

---
## Article 120 – Liability of Judges
---

(1) Judges are disciplinarily, civilly, and criminally liable, as applicable, for:  
a) bad faith;  
b) serious negligence;  
c) corruption;  
d) serious procedural violations;  
e) violation of incompatibilities.

(2) Judicial independence does not exempt from liability.

(3) Any mechanism of impunity is prohibited and is null and void by law.

---
## Article 121 – Judicial Discipline and Control of Disciplinary Decisions
---

(1) Judicial discipline is exercised by a specialized apolitical structure, established by organic law, whose members are selected exclusively through a public competition organized by ANMI.

(2) Disciplinary procedures are public as a rule, with strictly necessary exceptions for the protection of victims.

(3) Disciplinary decisions are mandatorily reasoned and subject to judicial review.

(4) Arbitrary or political disciplinary sanctions are prohibited and null and void by law.

---
## Article 122 – Digitalization of Justice and Traceability
---

(1) Justice is obliged to operate on a single digital platform for case files, records, and procedural communications.

(2) Any intervention in a case file must obligatorily leave complete and audited digital traces.

(3) The deletion, alteration, or concealment of digital traces is prohibited and criminally sanctioned.

(4) Digital systems may not substitute the judge; their role is strictly administrative and evidentiary, under human control.

---
## Article 123 – Access to Justice and Minimum Guarantees
---

(1) Access to justice is a guaranteed right and may not be restricted through excessive fees, opaque procedures, or arbitrary conditions.

(2) The State is obliged to ensure:  
a) clear and predictable procedures;  
b) reasonable time limits;  
c) accessible digital communication;  
d) legal assistance for persons without resources, under the conditions of the law.

(3) The unjustified refusal to receive an application or deliberate delay is prohibited and sanctioned.

(4) Any norm that renders access to justice illusory is null and void by law.

---
## Article 124 – Object of Constitutional Review
---

(1) Constitutional review is exercised over:  
a) laws and acts having the force of law;  
b) ordinances and normative acts of the Government;  
c) decisions and normative acts of central and local public authorities;  
d) parliamentary regulations;  
e) electoral procedures and referendums;  
f) constitutional conflicts between authorities.

(2) Any normative act or public procedure with general legal effect is mandatorily subject to constitutional review, directly or indirectly.

(3) The exclusion of constitutional review for any category of acts is prohibited, and the act of exclusion is null and void by law.

---
## Article 125 – Constitutional Court
---

(1) The Constitutional Court is the sole body of constitutional jurisdiction of the State.

(2) The Constitutional Court is composed of 5 constitutional judges.

(3) The Constitutional Court operates permanently and is obliged to ensure the resolution of referrals within reasonable time limits.

(4) Obstructing the functioning of the Constitutional Court constitutes a serious violation of the constitutional order and entails liability.

---
## Article 126 – Competences of the Constitutional Court
---

(1) The Constitutional Court has exclusive competence to:  
a) review the constitutionality of the acts provided for in Article 124;  
b) adjudicate exceptions of unconstitutionality raised before courts of law;  
c) adjudicate direct referrals regarding unconstitutionality;  
d) adjudicate constitutional conflicts between authorities;  
e) validate and constitutionally review electoral procedures and referendums.

(2) The exercise of the competences of the Constitutional Court by any other authority is prohibited.

(3) Exceeding its constitutional competences by the Court is prohibited and entails liability, under the conditions of this Constitution and the law.

---
## Article 127 – Right of Referral and Universal Access
---

(1) The right to refer matters to the Constitutional Court belongs to any person, natural or legal, and to any public authority or institution, without restrictions of procedural standing imposed by law.

(2) The exercise of the right of referral may not be conditioned upon prior authorizations, approvals, disproportionate fees, or political filters.

(3) Any act that restricts the right of referral is null and void by law.

---
## Article 128 – Technical Admissibility Filter
---

(1) In order to prevent abuse of referrals and to protect the working capacity of the Constitutional Court, a technical admissibility filter is established, with an exclusively procedural role.

(2) The filter may verify only:  
a) the existence of a determined subject matter and of an identifiable contested norm;  
b) the existence of a minimal, reasoned claim of unconstitutionality;  
c) the manifestly abusive or repetitive character of the referral, without new elements;  
d) compliance with essential formal requirements established by organic law.

(3) The filter may not rule on the merits and may not reject a referral that raises a plausible constitutional issue.

(4) The decision of inadmissibility shall be mandatorily:  
a) reasoned;  
b) published;  
c) subject to internal challenge before the Constitutional Court, within a time limit established by organic law.
(5) Members of the filter are selected exclusively through a public competition organized by ANMI, and any appointment without competition is null and void by law.

(6) Unlawful rejection of referrals entails disciplinary and criminal liability, as applicable.

---
## Article 129 – Selection of Constitutional Judges
---

(1) Constitutional judges are selected exclusively through a national public competition organized by ANMI.

(2) The competition is mandatorily:  
a) public;  
b) broadcast live;  
c) fully archived;  
d) technically audited;  
e) reasoned through verifiable scoring.

(3) Political, direct, discretionary, or institutionally negotiated appointment is absolutely prohibited.

(4) Appointment in violation of the competition is null and void by law, and acts signed by the unlawfully appointed person are voidable under the conditions of the law.

---
## Article 130 – Constitutional Eligibility Conditions
---

(1) The following are mandatory conditions for the office of constitutional judge:  
a) minimum age of 45 years;  
b) legal experience of at least 15 years;  
c) proven competence in constitutional law or public law;  
d) verified integrity;  
e) absence of final criminal convictions;  
f) strict apolitical character.

(2) Failure to fulfill any condition results in inadmissibility.

(3) Subsequent finding of ineligibility results in termination of the mandate by operation of law, under the conditions of the organic law.

---
## Article 131 – Mandate of Constitutional Judges
---

(1) The mandate of constitutional judges is 9 years, non-renewable.

(2) Renewal of the mandate, in any form, is prohibited and is null and void by law.

(3) The mandate terminates by operation of law upon expiration of the term, resignation, definitive impossibility, revocation for serious violations, or loss of eligibility.

---
## Article 132 – Quorum, Voting, and Anti-Deadlock Mechanism
---

(1) The Constitutional Court may deliberate only in the presence of a minimum of 4 judges.

(2) Decisions are adopted by the vote of a minimum of 3 judges.

(3) Deliberate absence of quorum is prohibited and constitutes a serious breach.

(4) In the event of repeated deadlock, the conditions for strictly procedural temporary replacement are established by organic law, without allowing political appointments.

---
## Article 133 – Constitutional Adjudication Procedure
---

(1) The procedure before the Constitutional Court is adversarial, contradictory, and guaranteed, under the conditions of the organic law.

(2) The Constitutional Court is obliged to ensure:  
a) the right of the parties to written and oral submissions;  
b) access to procedural documents;  
c) reasonable time limits;  
d) publication of procedural stages.

(3) The unjustified refusal to receive an admissible referral is prohibited and entails liability.

---
## Article 134 – Publicity, Reasoning, and Separate Opinions
---

(1) Decisions of the Constitutional Court are mandatorily reasoned, in fact and in law.

(2) The absence of reasoning or merely formal reasoning entails the nullity of the decision.

(3) Voting is nominal, and the opinion of each judge is published; separate and concurring opinions are permitted and mandatorily published.

(4) The publication of decisions and reasoning is mandatory in full, with the exception of strictly protected data.

---
## Article 135 – Effects of Decisions of the Constitutional Court
---

(1) Decisions of the Constitutional Court are binding on all public authorities, institutions, and persons.

(2) The normative act declared unconstitutional is:  
a) null and void by law, if the unconstitutionality concerns essential conditions; or  
b) inapplicable until remediation, if the Court expressly so orders, within the limits of the Constitution.

(3) Any authority is obliged to cease the application of the norm declared unconstitutional, immediately and in full.

(4) Refusal to comply constitutes a serious violation of the Constitution and entails liability.

---
## Article 136 – Time Limits and Constitutional Urgency
---

(1) The Constitutional Court is obliged to establish clear and public procedural time limits.

(2) Cases concerning fundamental rights, elections, or conflicts between authorities are adjudicated under a regime of constitutional urgency.

(3) Deliberate delay is prohibited and entails liability.

---
## Article 137 – Constitutional Conflicts Between Authorities
---

(1) Any conflict of constitutional competence between authorities is resolved exclusively by the Constitutional Court.

(2) Authorities involved in the conflict are obliged to provide the Court with all necessary documents; obstruction is prohibited.

(3) The decision of the Court establishes the obligations of compliance and the time limits, binding on all parties.

---
## Article 138 – Constitutional Review of Electoral Processes
---

(1) The Constitutional Court verifies the constitutionality of the electoral framework and the constitutional legality of electoral procedures.

(2) Serious violations that affect the outcome entail the annulment of the procedure, under the conditions of the organic law.

(3) Decisions of the Court in electoral matters are final and binding.

---
## Article 139 – Liability of Constitutional Judges
---

(1) Constitutional judges are personally liable for:  
a) bad faith;  
b) conflict of interest;  
c) corruption;  
d) serious procedural violations;  
e) serious violations of constitutional competence.

(2) Independence does not exempt from liability.

(3) Any mechanism of impunity is prohibited and is null and void by law.

---
## Article 140 – Incompatibilities and Prohibitions
---

(1) Constitutional judges may not exercise any other public or private function, remunerated or unremunerated, except for academic activities without conflict of interest.

(2) Political activity, affiliation, and propaganda are prohibited.

(3) Violation of incompatibilities results in termination of the mandate by operation of law.

---
## Article 141 – Revocation for Serious Violations
---

(1) The revocation of constitutional judges is permitted exclusively for serious violations of the Constitution, corruption, conflict of interest, or definitive incapacity.

(2) Revocation may not be politically ordered; the procedure is judicial and is established by organic law, with guarantees of impartiality.

(3) Arbitrary revocation is null and void by law.

---
## Article 142 – Protection of Petitioners and Prohibition of Retaliation
---

(1) Referral to the Constitutional Court may not entail retaliation, sanctions, or discrimination.

(2) Retaliation is prohibited and is sanctioned criminally and administratively, as applicable.

(3) The State is obliged to ensure effective mechanisms for the protection of petitioners.

---
## Article 143 – Technological Regime of Constitutional Procedures
---

(1) Digital systems used by the Court and by the technical filter are mandatorily audited, traceable, and publicly verifiable.

(2) Any alteration, deletion, or manipulation of procedural data is prohibited and criminally sanctioned.

(3) Technology may not substitute the deliberation or vote of constitutional judges.

---
## Article 144 – Relationship with Courts of Law
---

(1) Courts of law are obliged to apply the decisions of the Constitutional Court and to set aside from application the norms declared unconstitutional.

(2) Courts may raise exceptions of unconstitutionality ex officio or at the request of the parties, under the conditions of the organic law.

(3) The violation of the Court’s decisions by courts entails liability, under the conditions of the law.

---
## Article 145 – Obligation of Legislative Remediation
---

(1) Parliament and the Government are obliged to remedy the established unconstitutionality within the time limit set by the Court or, in its absence, within the time limit provided by the organic law.

(2) Formal or merely apparent remediation is prohibited.

(3) The deliberate maintenance of an unconstitutionality entails liability.

---
## Article 146 – Public Record of Constitutionality
---

(1) The Constitutional Court is obliged to maintain a public digital register of norms declared unconstitutional and of their effects.

(2) Failure to update the register is prohibited and entails administrative liability.

---
## Article 147 – Prohibition of Parallel Constitutional Jurisdictions
---

(1) The creation or functioning of a parallel constitutional jurisdiction is prohibited.

(2) Acts of a parallel jurisdiction are null and void by law, and the responsible persons incur legal liability.

---
## Article 148 – Budget and Functional Autonomy
---

(1) The Constitutional Court has its own budget, established by law, sufficient for its effective functioning.

(2) Intentional underfunding aimed at blocking the Court is prohibited and entails liability.

---
## Article 149 – Implementation Rules by Organic Law
---

(1) The organic law may regulate exclusively:  
a) the form and time limits of referrals;  
b) the procedure of the technical filter;  
c) the adjudication procedure;  
d) internal administrative organization;  
e) digital archiving, publication, and audit.

(2) The organic law may not restrict universal access, may not introduce political appointments, and may not diminish the effects of the Court’s decisions; any such provision is null and void by law.

---
## Article 150 – Safeguard Clause for Access and Review
---

(1) Any interpretation of norms regarding constitutional jurisdiction shall be made in the sense of guaranteeing universal access and the effective exercise of constitutional review.

(2) Any administrative or technological practice that renders access to the Court illusory is prohibited and entails the nullity of the respective acts.

Justice guarantees respect for the Constitution, the protection of citizens, and the rule of law.

# PART V – TITLE V: Economy
---

(Art. 151–180)

---
## PART V – ECONOMY
---

---
## Article 151 – National Economic System
---

(1) The economy of the State is founded on general economic balance, freedom of economic activity, guaranteed property, protected labor, and social responsibility.

(2) The State has the constitutional obligation to organize and maintain a stable, equitable, and predictable economic order, in the general interest of society.

(3) Any economic policy that produces serious structural imbalances, systemic exploitation, or unjustified concentration of economic power is prohibited.

(4) The violation of the constitutional economic order entails legal liability.

---
## Article 152 – Economic Freedom and Its Limits
---

(1) Economic activity is free and is exercised under the conditions of the law.

(2) Economic freedom may not be exercised abusively, to the detriment of:  
a) the public interest;  
b) economic security;  
c) the dignity of labor;  
d) fair competition.

(3) The State is obliged to intervene to correct serious economic dysfunctions.

(4) Arbitrary or preferential intervention is prohibited and sanctioned.

---
## Article 153 – Property in the Economic Order
---

(1) Public property and private property are guaranteed and protected equally.

(2) The exercise of the right of property entails economic and social responsibility.

(3) Expropriation is permitted only for a cause of public utility, with fair, prior, and full compensation.

(4) Indirect, disguised, or disproportionate interferences with property are prohibited and are null and void by law.

---
## Article 154 – Social Economy
---

(1) The social economy constitutes an integral part of the national economic order.

(2) The State is obliged to support economic mechanisms that:  
a) protect vulnerable persons;  
b) promote social inclusion;  
c) reduce excessive economic inequalities.

(3) Social economy programs are based on objective, verifiable, and non-discriminatory criteria.

(4) The use of the social economy for clientelist or political purposes is prohibited and sanctioned.

---
## Article 155 – Economic Security
---

(1) Economic security is a fundamental constitutional interest.

(2) The State has the obligation to protect the capacity of the economy to function continuously and stably.

(3) Acts that endanger the following are prohibited:  
a) financial stability;  
b) the functioning of essential economic infrastructures;  
c) the capacity of the State to fulfill its social obligations.

(4) The deliberate undermining of economic security entails serious liability.

---
## Article 156 – Competition and the Business Environment
---

(1) Fair competition is a mandatory constitutional principle.

(2) The State is obliged to ensure an open, predictable, and equitable business environment.

(3) The following are prohibited:  
a) cartels;  
b) unjustified monopolies;  
c) anti-competitive agreements;  
d) economic favoritism through public acts.
(4) The tolerance of anti-competitive practices entails institutional liability.

---
## Article 157 – Work and its economic role
---

(1) Work is a fundamental value of the economic order.

(2) The State is obliged to protect work against exploitation, extreme precarity, and economic abuse.

(3) Economic policies may not be constructed on the systemic depreciation of work.

(4) Violation of work protection entails legal sanctions.

---
## Article 158 – Economic and salary equity
---

(1) Economic equity constitutes a constitutional objective.

(2) Salary differentials are permitted exclusively on the basis of competence, responsibility, and performance.

(3) Salary discrimination, direct or indirect, is prohibited.

(4) The State is obliged to institute mechanisms for the control and correction of severe economic inequities.

---
## Article 159 – Transparency of economic activity
---

(1) Economic activity with public impact is mandatorily transparent.

(2) Public authorities and economic operators managing public funds have the obligation:
a) to make their activity public;
b) to ensure access to relevant economic information;
c) to allow public and institutional oversight.

(3) Unjustified economic opacity is prohibited.

(4) Digitalization is a mandatory instrument for achieving transparency, not an end in itself.

---
## Article 160 – Economic audit and control
---

(1) Public economic activity is subject to permanent economic control and audit.

(2) The audit has the following characteristics:
a) independent;
b) technical;
c) verifiable;
d) public.

(3) Obstruction of the economic audit constitutes a serious violation of the Constitution.

(4) Audit results produce mandatory legal effects.

---
## Article 161 – Fiscal responsibility
---

(1) The State may not adopt fiscal, budgetary, or debt policies that endanger the financial stability of the national economy.

(2) The adoption of fiscal measures without a public, verifiable, and auditable impact assessment is prohibited.

(3) The creation of hidden budgetary obligations, parallel funds, non-transparent guarantees, or unpublished extra-budgetary commitments is prohibited.

(4) Any fiscal or budgetary act adopted in violation of paragraphs (1)–(3) is null and void by law, and the responsible persons are liable financially, administratively, and criminally, as applicable.

---
## Article 162 – Public funds
---

(1) Public funds are managed exclusively under a regime of full transparency, complete traceability, and permanent audit.

(2) Any allocation, commitment, or payment from public funds is carried out only:
a) on the basis of an identifiable legal ground;
b) through a digital system;
c) with a complete digital trace;
d) with identification of the responsible decision-maker.

(3) The use of public funds outside their legal purpose is prohibited and entails full recovery of the damage and criminal liability, as applicable.

(4) The classification of the use of public funds is prohibited; acts of classification without constitutional grounds are null and void by law.

---
## Article 163 – Social economy
---

(1) The State is obliged to support social programs intended for the protection of vulnerable persons, under conditions of efficiency, traceability, and public oversight.

(2) Social programs are granted only on objective, verifiable, and non-discriminatory criteria; political or clientelist favoritism is prohibited.

(3) Any social program must be:
a) digitalized;
b) periodically audited;
c) publicly reported (criteria, budgets, results), with the protection of personal data.

(4) The diversion of social funds, simulation of eligibility, or unjustified obstruction of access for eligible persons constitutes a serious violation and is sanctioned.

---
## Article 164 – Economic security
---

(1) The State protects critical economic infrastructures and the continuity of essential economic functions.

(2) Critical economic infrastructures include, at a minimum: public payments, fiscal collection, budgetary systems, energy, water, transport, communications, and economic digital infrastructure.

(3) The capture, sabotage, manipulation, or compromise of critical economic infrastructures is prohibited and is sanctioned as a serious threat to the constitutional order.

(4) The State is obliged to ensure technical standards, redundancy, audit, and qualified personnel for the protection of these infrastructures.

---
## Article 165 – Work
---

(1) Work is free and is protected by the State.

(2) The State is obliged to guarantee all salaried citizens an income at least equal to the national average salary, defined and calculated by organic law in a transparent, uniform, and audited manner.

(3) The notion of “minimum salary” is prohibited in legislation and is mandatorily eliminated from domestic law.

(4) Exploitation, forced labor, abusive economic coercion, and any form of abuse against the worker are prohibited and sanctioned.

(5) Violation of the essential rights of workers entails the direct liability of the employer and of the officials who tolerate or conceal the abuse.

---
## Article 166 – Salary equality
---

(1) Salary differences are permitted exclusively on the basis of competence, merit, performance, and responsibility.

(2) Unjustified salary differences are prohibited.

(3) The State is obliged to prevent and sanction salary discrimination, direct or indirect, including through audit and control mechanisms.

(4) Contractual clauses or internal practices that produce salary discrimination are null and void by law.

---
## Article 167 – Remuneration of public functions
---

(1) No public function may have total remuneration greater than 2× the national average salary.

(2) Unjustified bonuses, benefits, and exceptional payments are prohibited.

(3) Strictly necessary logistical advantages (e.g. service vehicle, temporary accommodation) are permitted only if:
a) they are strictly regulated;
b) they are public;
c) they are functionally justified;
d) they are audited.

(4) Any payment or benefit granted in violation of paragraphs (1)–(3) is null, is fully recovered, and entails liability for the decision-makers.

---
## Article 168 – Protection of workers
---

(1) The State is obliged to ensure decent working conditions and protection for all workers.

(2) Minimum standards of occupational health and safety are mandatory and may not be diminished by contract or internal practices.

(3) Repeated or serious violation of worker protection entails administrative and criminal sanctions, as applicable.

---
## Article 169 – Social dialogue
---

(1) Employers’ organizations and trade unions may negotiate non-salary aspects, under the conditions of the law.

(2) Collective bargaining may not reduce the fundamental rights of workers and may not introduce discrimination.

(3) Any coercion, intimidation, or reprisals against participation in social dialogue are prohibited and sanctioned.

---
## Article 170 – Labor market
---

(1) The labor market is free, flexible, and minimally regulated, within the limits of worker protection and public order.

(2) Minimal regulation may not be used to eliminate basic protections; any such attempt is null and void by law.

(3) The State is obliged to prevent systemic exploitation and to ensure effective mechanisms of control and sanctioning.

---
## Article 171 – Economic development
---

(1) The State has the constitutional obligation to ensure sustainable, balanced, and inclusive economic development.

(2) Economic development policies must pursue:
a) the increase of productive capacity;
b) the valorization of internal resources;
c) the reduction of territorial economic disparities;
d) the creation of stable jobs.

(3) Economic development based on exploitation, structural imbalance, or excessive dependence is prohibited.

(4) The deliberate abandonment of regions or economic sectors constitutes a violation of the State’s constitutional obligations.

---
## Article 172 – Economic regions
---

(1) The State may organize economic regions for planning, coordination, and development.

(2) Economic regions have no political character and may not acquire political autonomy.

(3) The organization of economic regions is carried out exclusively on the basis of:
a) economic criteria;
b) logistical criteria;
c) demographic criteria;
d) functional criteria.

(4) The use of economic regions for political or clientelist purposes is prohibited.

---
## Article 173 – Natural resources
---

(1) Natural resources belong to the national patrimony.

(2) The exploitation of natural resources is carried out in the public interest, with respect for:
a) sustainability;
b) environmental protection;
c) equitable economic benefit.

(3) The concession or exploitation of natural resources is permitted only through transparent, competitive, and audited procedures.

(4) Fraudulent alienation or abusive exploitation of natural resources is null and void by law and sanctioned.

---
## Article 174 – Protection of the economic environment
---

(1) Environmental protection is an integral part of economic policy.

(2) Economic activity may not be carried out in violation of environmental standards.

(3) The polluter is obliged to bear in full the costs of remediation.

(4) The externalization of environmental costs to society is prohibited.

---
## Article 175 – Agriculture
---

(1) Agriculture is a strategic sector of the national economy.

(2) The State is obliged to ensure food security.

(3) The protection of agricultural land and domestic production is a constitutional obligation.

(4) The intentional destruction of agricultural capacity constitutes a serious violation.

---
## Article 176 – Industry
---

(1) The State supports industrial development with added value.

(2) Unjustified deindustrialization is prohibited.

(3) Industrial policies must pursue:
a) innovation;
b) efficiency;
c) competitiveness;
d) economic security.

(4) The abusive transfer of strategic industrial capacities is prohibited.

---
## Article 177 – Trade
---

(1) Trade is free, under the conditions of the law and of fair competition.

(2) The State protects domestic trade against unfair practices.

(3) Discriminatory trade treatments are prohibited.

(4) Trade must comply with economic, social, and environmental standards.

---
## Article 178 – Investments
---

(1) Lawful investments are guaranteed and protected.

(2) The State ensures equal treatment of investments, domestic and foreign.

(3) Investments that affect economic security, public order, or the national interest may be restricted by law.

(4) The arbitrary withdrawal of investment protection is prohibited.

---
## Article 179 – Financial stability
---

(1) The State has the obligation to maintain financial and monetary stability.

(2) Policies that generate excessive inflation or severe financial imbalances are prohibited.

(3) Public debt must be sustainable, transparent, and justified.

(4) The concealment of public debt or the manipulation of financial indicators is null and void by law.

---
## Article 180 – Purpose of the economic order
---

(1) The economic order is oriented toward:
a) general welfare;
b) the dignity of work;
c) social stability;
d) long-term economic security.

(2) The economy may not be organized exclusively in the interest of narrow groups.

(3) Any economic policy is interpreted in the sense of protecting the general interest and social balance.

The economy serves the welfare of the population, social equity, meritocracy, and sustainable development.

# PART VI – TITLE VI: National Security and Defense

(Art. 181–210)

---
## TITLE VI – NATIONAL SECURITY
---

---
## Article 181 – National security
---

(1) National security represents the state of protection of sovereignty, independence, constitutional order, territorial integrity, and the functional capacity of the State.
(2) National security has as its exclusive object:
a) the defense of the constitutional order;
b) the protection of the State against external aggression;
c) the prevention and combating of real and serious threats to the functioning of the State;
d) the protection of the population in situations of major risk.

(3) National security may not be invoked for:
a) the arbitrary restriction of fundamental rights and freedoms;
b) the repression of political or civic opposition;
c) the protection of private economic interests;
d) the concealment of illegalities, abuses, or acts of corruption;
e) the limitation of access to justice or to information of public interest.
(4) Any act, decision, or measure adopted under the pretext of national security, outside the limits provided in paragraph (2), is null and void by law.

(5) Public authorities have the constitutional obligation to demonstrate the real, necessary, and proportional character of any measure adopted in the field of national security.

(6) Violation of the provisions of the present article entails personal, legal, and constitutional liability for the responsible persons.

---
## Article 182 – Principles of national security
---

(1) National security is founded on the following mandatory constitutional principles:
a) the supremacy of the Constitution and the law;
b) democratic civilian control over all security and defense structures;
c) respect for fundamental rights and freedoms;
d) proportionality and necessity of measures;
e) responsibility and liability of authorities;
f) transparency compatible with the protection of strictly classified information.

(2) No principle of national security may be interpreted or applied in the sense of:
a) arbitrary restriction of fundamental rights;
b) circumvention of judicial control;
c) the creation of structures or practices parallel to the constitutional order.

(3) Violation of the principles provided in paragraph (1) entails the nullity of the adopted measures and the personal liability of the authors, under the conditions of the law.

(4) Any policy, strategy, or normative act in the field of national security is required to comply with the principles provided in the present article; the contrary is prohibited.

---
## Article 183 – Democratic civilian control
---

(1) All national security, defense, intelligence, and public order structures are subject to democratic civilian control, exercised effectively and continuously.

(2) Democratic civilian control is exercised through:
a) Parliament, through mechanisms of oversight, hearings, and budgetary control;
b) judicial courts, through the control of the legality of acts and measures;
c) civilian audit and control authorities, established by law, independent of the controlled structures.

(3) No security or defense structure may operate:
a) outside civilian control;
b) under exclusive military or intelligence authority;
c) on the basis of secret mandates not subject to judicial control.

(4) Refusal to submit to democratic civilian control, obstruction thereof, or the provision of false information constitutes a serious violation of the Constitution and entails legal liability.

(5) Democratic civilian control may not be suspended, limited, or replaced by invoking national security, a state of emergency, or any other exceptional situation.

(6) Their leadership is filled through ANMI competition, with a minimum age of 40 years.

---
## Article 184 – Prohibition of security abuse
---

The absolute prohibition of using security as a pretext for the unjustified limitation of fundamental rights.

---
## Chapter II – National Defense
---

---
## Article 185 – The role of the State in national security
---

(1) The State has the constitutional obligation to organize, coordinate, and exercise national security in a unitary, lawful, and controllable manner.

(2) The exercise of national security belongs exclusively to constitutional public authorities, within the limits of the competences established by the Constitution and the law.

(3) The creation, tolerance, or use of parallel, informal, or unconstitutional structures in the field of national security is prohibited.

(4) The deliberate inaction of the competent authorities in the face of real and serious threats to national security constitutes a violation of the Constitution and entails liability.

(5) Any measure in the field of national security must be:
a) necessary;
b) proportional;
c) temporary;
d) subject to civilian and judicial control.

---
## Article 186 – Missions of the armed forces
---

(1) The armed forces are the State institution intended for the armed defense of the State and act exclusively under the conditions of the Constitution and the law.

(2) The missions of the armed forces are limited to the following:
a) the defense of the sovereignty, independence, and territorial integrity of the State;
b) the protection of the population in situations of armed aggression, conflict, or serious threat;
c) intervention in situations of major crisis, disasters, or calamities, at the request of the competent civilian authorities;
d) participation in international missions, under the conditions of ratified treaties and in compliance with the Constitution.

(3) The armed forces may not exercise public order, investigative, political repression, or civilian administration powers, except in situations expressly provided by the Constitution.

(4) The use of the armed forces outside the missions provided in paragraph (2) is prohibited and constitutes a serious violation of the Constitution.

(5) Any order, directive, or command contrary to the present article is null and void by law, and its execution entails liability for both the author and the executor, under the conditions of the law.

---
## Article 187 – Political neutrality of the armed forces
---

(1) The armed forces are apolitical and are subordinated exclusively to the Constitution and to legitimate civilian authorities.

(2) Military personnel are prohibited from:
a) carrying out political activities;
b) being members of a political party or political formation;
c) participating in electoral campaigns;
d) holding elective or political offices during the period of military service.

(3) The use of military status to influence political life, directly or indirectly, is prohibited.

(4) Violation of the political neutrality of the armed forces constitutes a serious offense and entails disciplinary, administrative, or criminal liability, as applicable.

---
## Article 188 – Selection of military leadership
---

(1) All command and leadership positions within the armed forces are filled exclusively through a meritocratic competition, organized by the National Authority for Meritocracy and Integrity (ANMI).

(2) Appointment to command positions by delegation, political appointment, discretionary promotion, or any other procedure outside the competition is prohibited and null and void by law.

(3) The minimum constitutional conditions for holding command positions are:
a) a minimum age of 40 years;
b) relevant, proven military professional experience;
c) verified moral and professional integrity.

(4) The criteria provided in paragraph (3) are eliminatory in nature; failure to meet any of them entails automatic exclusion from the procedure.

(5) Competition procedures are public, transparent, and audited, within the limits compatible with national security.

(6) Violation of the rules for the selection of military leadership entails personal liability for the decision-makers involved.

---
## Article 189 – Training and equipping of the armed forces
---

(1) The State has the constitutional obligation to ensure the continuous, professional, and adequate training of the armed forces.

(2) Military training must be:
a) permanent;
b) adapted to real security risks;
c) compliant with assumed national and international standards;
d) periodically evaluated.

(3) The State is obliged to ensure the equipping of the armed forces with modern, functional equipment appropriate to the missions provided by the Constitution.

(4) Deliberate under-equipping, improper equipping, or the use of military resources for purposes other than defense constitutes a serious violation of constitutional obligations.

(5) Budgetary allocations for the training and equipping of the armed forces are subject to civilian control, audit, and transparency, within the limits compatible with national security.

---
## Chapter III – Public Order
---

---
## Article 190 – Public order institutions
---

(1) Public order institutions operate under the civilian authority of the Government, within the limits of the Constitution and the law.

(2) Public order institutions are apolitical and may not be used for political, electoral, or civic opposition repression purposes.

(3) The activity of public order institutions has as its object:
a) the maintenance of public order;
b) the protection of persons and property;
c) the prevention and combating of antisocial acts, under the conditions of the law.

(4) Public order institutions are obliged to respect fundamental rights and freedoms; their violation entails personal liability.

---
## Article 191 – Protection of the population
---

(1) The State has the constitutional obligation to ensure the protection of the population against risks to life, integrity, and safety.

(2) Protection of the population is carried out through:
a) rapid and coordinated interventions;
b) efficient early warning systems;
c) prevention and crisis response infrastructure.

(3) The absence, malfunctioning, or deliberate failure to update protection mechanisms constitutes a serious violation of the State’s obligations.

---
## Article 192 – Transparency of public order operations
---

(1) Public order operations are mandatorily documented, including through digital means, under the conditions of the law.

(2) Documentation has as its purpose:
a) ensuring the legality of interventions;
b) the protection of individual rights;
c) subsequent verification of the manner of action.

(3) Excessive use of force is prohibited and is sanctioned disciplinarily, administratively, or criminally, as applicable.

(4) The lack of documentation or its alteration constitutes a serious offense and entails liability.

---
## Chapter IV – Digital Security
---

---
## Article 193 – Critical digital infrastructures
---

(1) Critical digital infrastructures constitute essential elements of national security.

(2) The State has the constitutional obligation to identify, protect, and maintain the functionality of critical digital infrastructures.

(3) The protection of critical digital infrastructures is carried out through:
a) strict and up-to-date technical standards;
b) prevention, detection, and response measures;
c) redundancy and operational continuity.

(4) Neglect, under-protection, or deliberate exposure of critical digital infrastructures constitutes a serious violation of national security.

---
## Article 194 – Cyber attacks
---

(1) Cyber attacks directed against the State, public institutions, or critical digital infrastructures constitute serious threats to national security.

(2) The State is entitled to adopt defensive measures and countermeasures, subject cumulatively to the following conditions:
a) legality;
b) necessity;
c) proportionality;
d) civilian and judicial control.

(3) Countermeasures that affect the fundamental rights of uninvolved persons or that exceed the defensive purpose are prohibited.

---
## Article 195 – National Authority for Cybersecurity
---

(1) The National Authority for Cybersecurity (ANSC) is an independent, apolitical public institution with functional autonomy.

(2) ANSC coordinates digital security at the national level, without substituting the competences of other public authorities.

(3) The leadership of ANSC is selected exclusively through a meritocratic competition, organized by ANMI, with compliance with conditions of integrity, competence, and a minimum age of 45 years.

(4) ANSC may not carry out activities of political or economic surveillance or information content control.

(5) The activity of ANSC is subject to civilian control, audit, and transparency, within the limits compatible with national security.

---
## Article 196 – Digital security standards
---

(1) All public institutions have the constitutional obligation to implement and maintain digital security protocols adequate to existing risks.

(2) Private operators managing critical digital infrastructures or data of public interest are subject to the same obligations.

(3) Digital security standards are established by law, are mandatory, and are subject to periodic audit.

(4) Failure to comply with digital security standards entails legal liability.

---
## Chapter V – Intelligence Services
---

---
## Article 197 – Role of the intelligence services
---

(1) Intelligence services have as their exclusive role the protection of national security, within the strict limits established by the Constitution and the law.

(2) The activity of intelligence services is strictly regulated and subject to democratic civilian control, parliamentary control, and judicial control.

(3) Intelligence services may not act outside the legal framework and may not exercise competences belonging to other public authorities.

(4) Any activity carried out outside the role provided in paragraph (1) is prohibited and entails legal liability.

---
## Article 198 – Selection of the leadership of intelligence services
---

(1) The heads of the intelligence services are selected exclusively through a public meritocratic competition, organized by the National Authority for Meritocracy and Integrity (ANMI).

(2) The minimum constitutional conditions for holding the position are:
a) a minimum age of 40 years;
b) proven professional competence;
c) verified moral and professional integrity.

(3) The mandate of the leadership of the intelligence services is 6 years, without the possibility of renewal.

(4) Appointment outside the procedure provided in paragraph (1) is null and void by law.

---
## Article 199 – Strict prohibitions on intelligence services
---

(1) Intelligence services are prohibited from:
a) intervening in political activity;
b) influencing political, electoral, or administrative processes;
c) carrying out economic or commercial activities;
d) constituting or tolerating parallel structures of power.

(2) Surveillance of persons is permitted exclusively on the basis of a judicial warrant, issued under the conditions of the law.

(3) Any activity of tracking, data collection, or influence carried out outside the legal framework is prohibited and sanctioned.

---
## Article 200 – Parliamentary control over intelligence services
---

(1) Parliament exercises periodic and effective control over the activity of intelligence services.

(2) Parliamentary control includes:
a) verification of the legality of activities;
b) budgetary control;
c) evaluation of compliance with fundamental rights.

(3) Intelligence services have the obligation to submit annual reports regarding their activity.

(4) The reports are public, except for information strictly necessary for the protection of national security, duly motivated and justified.

(5) Unjustified refusal to submit to parliamentary control constitutes a serious violation of the Constitution.

---
## Chapter VI – States of Emergency
---

---
## Article 201 – Declaration of the state of emergency
---

(1) The state of emergency may be declared exclusively in extreme situations that seriously endanger the life of the population, the constitutional order, or the functioning of the State.

(2) The declaration of the state of emergency is made only by Parliament, upon the reasoned proposal of the Government.

(3) The act of declaration must explicitly specify:
a) the nature of the situation justifying the measure;
b) the territory of application;
c) the determined duration;
d) the concrete measures ordered.

(4) The measures adopted during the state of emergency must be strictly necessary, proportional, and temporary.

(5) The restriction of fundamental rights is permitted only within the limits of the Constitution and may not affect essential rights.

---
## Article 202 – Mobilization
---

General or partial mobilization of the armed forces may be decided exclusively by Parliament, under the conditions provided by the Constitution and the law.

---
## Article 203 – State of war
---

The declaration of a state of war is made exclusively by Parliament, following the ascertainment of an armed aggression or a serious imminent threat against the State.

---
## Article 204 – Limitation of exceptional measures
---

(1) Exceptional measures cease by operation of law once the situation that justified them disappears.

(2) The unjustified extension or maintenance of exceptional measures is prohibited and constitutes a serious violation of the Constitution.

(3) All exceptional measures are subject to parliamentary and judicial control.

---
## Chapter VII – International Cooperation
---

---
## Article 205 – External cooperation in security matters
---

The State cooperates with other States and international organizations in the field of security, with respect for national sovereignty, the Constitution, and the national interest.

---
## Article 206 – Participation in international missions
---

Participation of the armed forces in international missions or operations is carried out only with the approval of Parliament, under the conditions of ratified treaties and of the Constitution.

---
## Article 207 – International exchange of information
---

(1) The international exchange of information and data is carried out exclusively on the basis of treaties, agreements, or laws in force.

(2) The exchange of information is permitted only with strict guarantees regarding the protection of citizens’ rights and freedoms.

(3) The transfer of data that affects fundamental rights, in the absence of legal guarantees, is prohibited.

---
## Chapter VIII – Critical Infrastructures
---

---
## Article 208 – Definition of critical infrastructures
---

(1) Critical infrastructures are those essential systems and services whose functioning is indispensable for national security and the life of society.

(2) Critical infrastructures include, at a minimum, the following sectors:
energy, water, healthcare, transport, communications, digital infrastructure, defense, and other sectors established by law.

---
## Article 209 – Protection of critical infrastructures
---

(1) The State has the constitutional obligation to protect critical infrastructures.

(2) Protection is carried out through:
a) modern security standards;
b) qualified personnel;
c) prevention, intervention, and continuity mechanisms.

(3) Neglect or compromise of critical infrastructures constitutes a serious violation of the State’s obligations.

---
## Article 210 – Purpose of national security
---

National security has as its purpose the protection of the individual, society, and the constitutional order, and may not be used as an instrument of political, economic, or administrative domination.

# PART VII – TITLE VII: Revision of the Constitution
---

(Art. 211–225)

---
## Article 211 – Principle of constitutional revision
---

(1) The Constitution may be revised exclusively for adaptation to the evolution of society, with strict respect for fundamental constitutional limits.

(2) Revision may not have as its purpose the arbitrary restriction of rights, institutional capture, or alteration of the constitutional order.

---
## Article 212 – Initiative of revision
---

The initiative for revising the Constitution may belong to:
a) one third of the total number of members of Parliament;
b) the Government;
c) a number of at least 3% of citizens with voting rights;
d) the National Authority for Meritocracy and Integrity (ANMI), exclusively in the fields of meritocracy, public ethics, and institutional integrity.

---
## Article 213 – Parliamentary procedure
---

(1) The proposal for revision is adopted by Parliament with a vote of two thirds of the total number of its members.

(2) The adopted text is automatically and mandatorily transmitted to the constitutional referendum procedure.

---
## Article 214 – Mandatory digital referendum
---

(1) Revision of the Constitution is mandatorily subject to a national referendum.

(2) The referendum is conducted exclusively in digital format, on secure, audited, and verifiable infrastructure, including through blockchain technologies.

(3) The result of the referendum is final and binding.

(4) Participation of adult citizens in the referendum constitutes a civic obligation.

---
## Article 215 – Limits of revision
---

The following may not be subject to revision:
a) the sovereign, unitary, and indivisible character of the State;
b) fundamental rights and freedoms;
c) human dignity;
d) meritocracy as a constitutional principle;
e) the apolitical nature of State institutions;
f) the supremacy of the Constitution;
g) the prohibition of technological domination over the individual;
h) the independence of the judiciary;
i) the transparency of judicial proceedings;
j) the guarantee of the national average salary as a minimum level;
k) the elimination of the office of President.

---
## Article 216 – Constitutional review of revision
---

The Constitutional Court exercises exclusive control over the constitutionality of the revision procedure, without substituting the popular will expressed through the referendum.

---
## Article 217 – Entry into force of the revision
---

The revision of the Constitution enters into force on the date of publication of the referendum results.

---
## Article 218 – Harmonization of legislation
---

Legislation in force shall be harmonized with the revised Constitution within a maximum of 12 months from its entry into force.

---
## Article 219 – Prohibition of revision in exceptional situations
---

The Constitution may not be revised during:
– a state of emergency;
– mobilization;
– a state of war.

---
## Article 220 – Constitutional continuity
---

No constitutional revision may produce a power vacuum or the suspension of the functioning of State institutions.

---
## Article 221 – Civic participation
---

Citizens have the right to formulate and submit proposals for constitutional revision, under the conditions of the law.

---
## Article 222 – Prohibition of modification through inferior acts
---

The Constitution may not be modified, supplemented, or suspended by law, ordinance, decision, or regulation.

---
## Article 223 – Constitutional stability
---

Revisions of the Constitution may not be frequent and may not have the effect of destabilizing institutions or the constitutional order.

---
## Article 224 – Publicity of the revision procedure
---

The entire process of constitutional revision is public, transparent, and digitalized, with respect for the security of the process.

---
## Article 225 – Purpose of revision
---

Revision of the Constitution must pursue the consolidation of democracy, meritocracy, and respect for fundamental rights.

---
# PART VIII – TITLE VIII: Final and Transitional Provisions
---

(Art. 226–250)

---
## Article 226 – Direct application of the Constitution
---

The Constitution is applied directly and with priority. In the event of normative conflict, the Constitution prevails absolutely.

---
## Article 227 – Validity of prior normative acts
---

Prior normative acts remain applicable only insofar as they do not contravene the present Constitution.

---
## Article 228 – Repeal of contrary provisions
---

Legal provisions contrary to the present Constitution are repealed by law.

---
## Article 229 – Institutional transition
---

(1) All State institutions have the obligation to align with the Constitution within a maximum of 12 months.

(2) The transition may not affect the continuity of public services.

---
## Article 230 – Gradual digitalization
---

(1) The digitalization of public institutions is carried out progressively and in a controlled manner.

(2) Algorithmic systems may not adopt decisions without human verification and responsibility.

(3) Digital traceability of public decisions is mandatory.

---
## Article 231 – Transfer of competences
---

Newly created institutions take over the competences of reorganized ones without interruption of the functioning of the State.

---
## Article 232 – Staff stability
---

(1) Existing staff retain their positions until reorganization.

(2) Subsequently, positions are filled exclusively through public competition.

---
## Article 233 – International treaties
---

The State respects international treaties compatible with the Constitution.

---
## Article 234 – Supreme interpretation
---

In the event of an interpretative conflict, the interpretation that protects the dignity and freedom of the individual prevails.

---
## Article 235 – Absolute priority of the human person
---

The human person has absolute priority over technology, algorithmic systems, and non-human entities.

---
## Article 236 – Regime of emerging entities
---

Non-human entities may not hold legal, political, or administrative rights.

---
## Article 237 – Technological assessment
---

A special law establishes the criteria for assessing technological maturity for the use of algorithmic systems.

---
## Article 238 – Name of the Constitution
---

The present fundamental law is named the Constitution of AlgoraHumani.

---
## Article 239 – Official publication
---

The Constitution is published in the Official Gazette and on the national digital platform.

---
## Article 240 – Accessibility
---

The text of the Constitution is accessible free of charge to all citizens.

---
## Article 241 – Official language
---

The Constitution is drafted and applied in the official language of the State.

---
## Article 242 – Publicity of constitutional acts
---

Any constitutional amendment is published in full.

---
## Article 243 – Responsibility of authorities
---

Public authorities have the constitutional obligation to respect the constitutional order.

---
## Article 244 – Liability for violation of the Constitution
---

(1) Deliberate violation of the Constitution entails legal liability.

(2) Serious violation entails the automatic loss of public office.

---
## Article 245 – Constitutional oath
---

Persons holding public office take an oath to respect the Constitution.

---
## Article 246 – Prohibition of derogation
---

No law or administrative act may derogate from the Constitution.

---
## Article 247 – Constitutional order
---

The State guarantees the continuous functioning of the constitutional order.

---
## Article 248 – Constitutional archive
---

The State maintains the digital archive of all versions of the Constitution and its revisions.

---
## Article 249 – Binding force
---

The Constitution has supreme legal force and is binding on all persons and institutions.

---
## Article 250 – Entry into force
---

The present Constitution enters into force 30 days after its official publication.
